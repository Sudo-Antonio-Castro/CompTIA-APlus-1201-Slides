CompTIA A+ 220-1201

Networking

LAN, WAN, WLAN, MAN, PAN, SAN

  • LAN (Local Area Network): connects devices within a building or campus using Ethernet and/or Wi-Fi; typically high speed and privately managed.
  • WAN (Wide Area Network): connects multiple LANs over large distances using service-provider links; bandwidth, cost, and latency are bigger factors than on a LAN.
  • WLAN (Wireless LAN): a LAN delivered over Wi-Fi via access points; coverage, interference, and capacity planning matter as much as raw speed.
  • MAN (Metropolitan Area Network): connects sites across a city or metro area, often via provider fiber; operationally similar to WAN because provider infrastructure is involved.
  • PAN (Personal Area Network): short-range networking around a person — Bluetooth peripherals, phone tethering — prioritizes convenience and low power.
  • SAN (Storage Area Network): a dedicated high-speed network for storage traffic, common in servers and data centers; designed for reliability and predictable performance.
Summary

Network types are mainly defined by geographic scope and purpose. LANs and WLANs serve local users, while WANs and MANs connect multiple locations through longer-distance links. PANs handle very short-range device connectivity, and SANs isolate storage traffic to keep servers fast and stable. Picking the right network type helps you set realistic expectations for speed, reliability, and troubleshooting.

A+ Exam Tip

Know each network type by its scope: PAN = personal (Bluetooth), LAN = local building, MAN = city, WAN = across regions, SAN = dedicated storage network. The exam often asks you to match a scenario to the correct type.

SOHO and Enterprise Networks

  • SOHO (Small Office/Home Office) networks are designed for simplicity: a single router, basic switching, and minimal segmentation are common.
  • SOHO focuses on affordability and ease of setup, but may have limited redundancy, monitoring, and security controls compared to larger environments.
  • Enterprise networks prioritize scalability, security, and uptime with managed switches, VLANs, centralized authentication, and documented standards.
  • Enterprise designs often include redundancy (multiple switches and links), monitoring and alerting, and standardized cabling and rack layouts to reduce downtime.
Summary

SOHO networks emphasize easy installation and low cost, which is why they often rely on all-in-one gear and minimal configuration. Enterprise networks trade simplicity for control by using managed infrastructure, segmentation, and centralized policies. Redundancy and monitoring are common in enterprise environments because downtime is expensive. Understanding the environment helps you predict what equipment and troubleshooting processes to expect.

A+ Exam Tip

SOHO = simple, affordable, all-in-one. Enterprise = managed, segmented, redundant. The exam may describe a scenario and ask which type it describes, or which features belong to each.

Data Centers

  • Data centers host critical services — servers, storage, virtualization — and are built for high availability using redundant power, cooling, and network paths.
  • Network design typically includes segmentation (separating management, server, storage, and user traffic) to improve security and performance.
  • Throughput and low latency between systems is a priority; structured cabling, labeling, and change control reduce operational risk.
  • Support work often involves monitoring utilization, validating failover behavior, and maintaining consistent documentation across racks and links.
Summary

Data centers are engineered for reliability because they run the systems organizations depend on. They use redundancy and segmentation so failures don't take down the entire environment. Performance matters inside the data center, especially between servers and storage. Clear cabling standards and documentation are essential so changes and troubleshooting don't introduce new outages.

Network Interface Cards (NICs)

  • A NIC is the hardware that connects a host to a network segment, providing the physical interface and link signaling.
  • Wired NICs commonly use an RJ-45 port for twisted-pair Ethernet, while fiber NICs use optical ports and appropriate transceivers.
  • NICs have a unique MAC address that is used for local network communication and switching decisions.
  • Common troubleshooting includes checking link lights, cable and port health, driver status, and matching speed and duplex settings.
Summary

A NIC is the component that lets a device join a network and exchange frames on the local link. Ethernet NICs usually connect via RJ-45 copper, while higher-distance or higher-interference environments may use fiber. Because the NIC's MAC address identifies it on the local network, it plays a key role in how switches deliver traffic. Most NIC issues are resolved by validating the physical link first, then drivers and configuration.

A+ Exam Tip

NICs have a MAC address burned in, used for local switching decisions. Troubleshoot in order: link lights → cable → driver → speed/duplex mismatch. A duplex mismatch causes slow speeds and high errors even when the link light is on.

Patch Panels

  • A patch panel is a central termination point for structured cabling, letting permanent in-wall cables end cleanly in a rack or wiring closet.
  • Devices connect to the patch panel using short patch cords, which reduces wear on permanent cabling and makes changes easier.
  • Patch panels improve organization: labeling and consistent port mapping make troubleshooting and moves/adds/changes faster.
  • They are commonly used in office LANs and data centers to keep cabling manageable at scale.
Summary

Patch panels make structured cabling practical by providing a stable, labeled termination point in a closet or rack. Instead of moving permanent cables around, you change short patch cords to redirect connections. This reduces damage risk and improves airflow and organization. In troubleshooting, patch panels help you quickly trace which wall port or cable run maps to a specific switch port.

Switches and MAC Address Learning

  • A switch connects multiple devices on a LAN and forwards frames based on MAC addresses rather than broadcasting everything.
  • When a switch receives a frame, it learns the source MAC address and stores it in a MAC address table mapped to the ingress port.
  • For known destinations, the switch forwards only to the correct port; for unknown destinations, it floods frames until it learns the correct path.
  • This behavior reduces unnecessary traffic compared to hubs and improves overall LAN efficiency.

Managed vs Unmanaged

  • An unmanaged switch works out of the box with no configuration — good for small networks where simplicity is the priority.
  • A managed switch supports configuration and monitoring: VLANs (traffic separation), QoS (quality of service), and port monitoring for troubleshooting.
  • In enterprise environments, managed switches help with scalability, security segmentation, and performance tuning.
Summary

Switches improve LAN performance by forwarding frames only where they need to go using a MAC address table. Unmanaged switches are simple and inexpensive for small setups. Managed switches add control and visibility that become essential as networks grow. VLANs and QoS allow you to separate traffic types and prioritize important services.

A+ Exam Tip

Switches forward based on MAC addresses; routers forward based on IP addresses. Unmanaged = plug and play, no config. Managed = VLANs, QoS, monitoring. Know the difference — the exam tests both.

Power over Ethernet (PoE)

  • PoE lets network devices receive electrical power over Ethernet cabling, reducing the need for nearby power outlets.
  • Common PoE standards: 802.3af (up to 15.4 W), 802.3at (up to 25.5 W), and 802.3bt (up to 60 W or 100 W depending on type).
  • PoE is frequently used for access points, IP phones, and cameras — especially where clean installs and central power management matter.
  • When troubleshooting PoE, verify switch/injector capability, cable quality, and that the powered device's wattage matches the PoE budget.
Summary

PoE simplifies deployments by sending both data and power through the same Ethernet run. Different PoE standards support different wattage levels, so matching the device requirement to the switch capability is critical. Most PoE issues come down to insufficient power budget, incompatible standards, or cabling problems.

A+ Exam Tip

Know the three PoE standards and their wattages: 802.3af = 15.4 W, 802.3at = 25.5 W, 802.3bt = up to 100 W. A device that randomly reboots or won't power on via PoE usually means the switch port doesn't have enough wattage for that device.

Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP)

  • UTP is the most common Ethernet cabling in homes and offices — flexible, cost-effective, and easy to terminate.
  • Twisting the wire pairs helps reduce crosstalk and improves signal quality without requiring metal shielding.
  • Structured cabling designs typically target a maximum channel length of about 100 meters for copper Ethernet.
  • UTP works best when routed away from high-EMI sources and installed with proper bend radius and termination practices.

Shielded Twisted Pair (STP)

  • Shielded cabling adds foil and/or braided screens to reduce EMI and crosstalk in noisy environments (industrial spaces, near motors, etc.).
  • Common styles: shielding around the whole cable, around each pair, or both — for example F/UTP, U/FTP, S/FTP.
  • Shielding must be properly bonded and grounded through connectors and patch panels, or it can act like an antenna and worsen interference.
Summary

UTP is the default choice for most Ethernet runs because it provides strong performance at reasonable cost. STP is used when electrical noise could disrupt reliable Ethernet signaling. The key tradeoff is cost and installation complexity versus better noise protection. Proper grounding is required for shielded designs to work as intended — improper grounding can make interference worse.

A+ Exam Tip

UTP = standard office and home cabling. STP = noisy environments like factories. Max copper Ethernet run = 100 meters. If STP is not properly grounded it can make interference worse — the exam may test this gotcha.

Connectors

  • RJ-45 is the standard modular connector for Ethernet over twisted pair; it must match the wiring standard (T568A or T568B) end-to-end.
  • Keystone jacks are used in wall plates; cables are terminated on the back (often via punch-down IDC) and patched on the front.
  • Patch panels provide a central termination point so permanent cabling stays fixed while short patch cords handle changes.
  • Connector quality and termination technique — keeping pair twists intact close to the connector end — directly affect link stability at higher speeds.

Installation Tools

  • Crimper: attaches modular RJ-45 connectors by pressing pins into the conductors for proper electrical contact.
  • Punch-down tool: terminates wires into insulation displacement connectors (IDC) on jacks and patch panels.
  • Cable stripper/cutter: prepares cable jackets cleanly without nicking conductors (nicked conductors cause intermittent faults).

Test Tools

  • Cable tester: checks continuity and pinout to catch opens, shorts, crossed pairs, and split pairs.
  • Tone generator & probe: helps identify which cable run maps to a wall port or patch panel position.
  • Loopback plug: sends transmitted signals back to the receiver to validate a port or NIC during troubleshooting.
  • Network tap: copies traffic from a live link for monitoring and analysis without disrupting the connection.
Summary

Copper Ethernet relies on consistent connectors and clean terminations to meet performance targets. Using the same wiring standard end-to-end and keeping twists intact near terminations reduces crosstalk. Many "mystery" cable issues trace back to rushed or low-quality terminations. Test tools let you prove whether cabling is wired correctly — a basic tester quickly catches wiring mistakes after termination.

A+ Exam Tip

Crimper = RJ-45 ends. Punch-down tool = keystone jacks and patch panels. Tone generator = tracing cable runs. Cable tester = verifying pinout. Loopback plug = testing the port itself. Know which tool goes with which job.

Optical (Fiber) Cabling

  • Fiber carries data as light, making it immune to EMI and ideal for long distances and high-bandwidth uplinks and backbones.
  • Single-mode (SMF): supports very long distances (kilometers); uses a smaller core and typically a laser light source.
  • Multimode (MMF): common for shorter runs inside buildings and data centers; uses a larger core and LED or VCSEL light source.
  • Common connectors include LC (small form, common in data centers), SC (square push-pull), and ST (twist-lock, older).
  • Fiber links often use transceivers (e.g., SFP modules) matched to speed, wavelength, and fiber type.
  • Clean connectors and proper handling are critical — contamination and stress are common causes of signal attenuation.
Summary

Fiber is chosen when distance, bandwidth, or electrical noise makes copper impractical. Single-mode and multimode support different distance and equipment requirements, so matching transceivers and connectors is essential. Fiber is also sensitive to physical stress and dirty connectors, which can create intermittent or weak links. Good cleaning and handling practices are a major part of reliable fiber support.

A+ Exam Tip

Single-mode = long distance (buildings, campuses, WAN). Multimode = short distance (inside a building or data center). A dirty or cracked fiber connector is the most common cause of high attenuation — always inspect and clean before replacing hardware.

Coaxial Cabling

  • Coax uses a central conductor with shielding around it, making it effective for RF signals and helping block interference.
  • Common for broadband Internet (cable modem handoffs), some video distribution, and certain security and media applications.
  • Install quality depends on tight, properly terminated connectors and correct splitter usage to avoid signal loss and noise.
  • Troubleshooting often includes checking connector ends, splitters, cable damage, and signal levels from the provider or source equipment.
Summary

Coax is still widely used for Internet service delivery even when endpoint LANs are Ethernet. Its shielding helps protect RF signals, but connectors and splitters can easily degrade performance if installed poorly. Physical damage and loose connections are common causes of signal issues. When speeds or stability are poor, checking the coax path end-to-end is usually the fastest way to find the problem.

Copper Cabling Installation Considerations

  • Plenum-rated cable is required in air-handling spaces (above drop ceilings used for HVAC return air); it produces less smoke and fewer toxic fumes in a fire compared to standard PVC jackets.
  • Route copper away from high-EMI sources (fluorescent lights, motors, elevator shafts), avoid tight bends, and protect cables from pinches and heavy compression.
  • Maintain proper bend radius and don't untwist pairs more than necessary near terminations to preserve performance at higher speeds.
  • Label and document all runs (wall port → patch panel port → switch port) so troubleshooting never requires guesswork.
Summary

A copper link can test "good" on day one and still fail later if it's routed poorly or stressed mechanically. Environment and jacket rating matter, especially in plenum spaces and areas with electrical noise. Termination technique is critical for higher speeds because small mistakes increase crosstalk. Documentation and labeling are the difference between a fast fix and hours of tracing.

A+ Exam Tip

Plenum vs riser vs PVC is a common exam topic. Plenum = air-handling spaces (required by code). Riser = vertical runs between floors. PVC = general use where neither of the above applies. Using the wrong jacket type in a plenum space is a code violation.

Access Points (APs)

  • Most Wi-Fi networks use infrastructure mode, where clients connect through an AP rather than directly to each other.
  • The AP bridges wireless clients to the wired LAN and handles client authentication and association to the SSID.
  • In business environments, APs are commonly powered via PoE so they can be mounted where coverage is best — often on ceilings.
  • Troubleshooting often starts with verifying SSID settings, channel and band selection, and whether the AP is properly connected to the wired network.
Summary

Access points are the core building block of most Wi-Fi networks because they centralize connectivity and management. They connect wireless clients to the wired LAN and control how devices join the network. PoE makes AP placement easier, which improves coverage and user experience. Many Wi-Fi problems are solved by validating AP configuration and its wired uplink first.

A+ Exam Tip

Infrastructure mode (clients go through an AP) is the standard for business Wi-Fi. Ad-hoc mode (devices connect directly to each other, no AP) is rare and mostly tested as a contrast. When troubleshooting Wi-Fi, always check the AP's wired connection before assuming a wireless problem.

Frequency Bands

  • 2.4 GHz: best range and wall penetration, but crowded and more interference-prone (Bluetooth, microwaves, neighboring Wi-Fi).
  • 5 GHz: more channels and typically better throughput, but shorter range than 2.4 GHz.
  • 6 GHz: newest band (Wi-Fi 6E / Wi-Fi 7) with lots of clean spectrum and high performance, but shortest range.
  • Band choice is a tradeoff between coverage, capacity, and interference; dual/tri-band APs help distribute clients effectively.
Summary

Wi-Fi performance is heavily influenced by which band clients use. 2.4 GHz travels farther but is often noisy, while 5 GHz usually offers better capacity and less interference. 6 GHz provides even more usable spectrum for dense environments, but it won't reach as far. Understanding band tradeoffs helps explain why a strong signal isn't always a fast connection.

A+ Exam Tip

2.4 GHz = long range, more interference. 5 GHz = shorter range, less interference, more channels. 6 GHz = newest, cleanest, shortest range. A user far from the AP on 5 GHz may actually do better on 2.4 GHz even though 5 GHz is "faster."

Wi-Fi 6, 6E, and Wi-Fi 7

  • Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) improves efficiency in crowded networks with OFDMA — it splits a channel so multiple clients share it at once, reducing wait time when many devices connect.
  • Wi-Fi 6E extends Wi-Fi 6 into the 6 GHz band, adding more clean spectrum and reducing congestion in high-density environments.
  • Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be) adds MLO (Multi-Link Operation) — devices can use multiple bands simultaneously, reducing latency and improving stability when one band is congested.
  • Wi-Fi 7 supports very wide 320 MHz channels in 6 GHz, enabling extremely high throughput for demanding use cases.
  • To benefit from these features, both the client device and the AP must support them.
Summary

Wi-Fi 6 focuses on efficiency when many devices are connected, so performance stays consistent under load — not just when you're the only one using it. Wi-Fi 6E adds the 6 GHz band to reduce congestion further. Wi-Fi 7 adds multi-band operation and wider channels for the highest-demand environments. Choosing between them depends on client device support and how dense the environment is.

Wireless LAN Installation Considerations

  • Plan SSIDs and security settings intentionally: consistent naming, modern encryption (WPA3 where supported), and clear guest vs internal network separation.
  • Decide band strategy — same SSID across bands vs separate SSIDs — based on user needs and device roaming behavior.
  • AP placement matters: ceilings, central locations, and avoiding heavy obstructions improve coverage. Too much transmit power can cause sticky-client roaming problems where devices refuse to roam to a closer AP.
  • Channel planning and interference awareness reduce drops and slowdowns — measure and adjust instead of guessing.

Wi-Fi Analyzers

  • Wi-Fi analyzers measure signal strength (dBm), channel utilization, and interference sources to support better placement and tuning.
  • They help identify congestion (too many networks on the same channel) and coverage gaps (weak signal zones).
  • Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is critical for stability — a strong signal doesn't help if noise is also high.
  • Analyzer data supports changes like moving APs, adjusting channels, reducing overlap, or steering clients to less congested bands.
Summary

Wireless success is mostly design and placement, not just buying a faster router. SSID and security decisions affect usability and risk, while band strategy influences roaming and performance. Good AP placement and sensible power levels improve real-world coverage more than raw advertised speeds. Wi-Fi analyzers make troubleshooting measurable instead of guess-based — they show where signal is weak and where channels are crowded.

A+ Exam Tip

The exam tests channel overlap on 2.4 GHz — only channels 1, 6, and 11 are non-overlapping. For sticky client issues (device stays on a far AP instead of roaming to a closer one), the fix is often reducing AP transmit power, not increasing it.

Bluetooth, RFID, and NFC

  • Bluetooth: short-range (typically up to 10 m, class-dependent) wireless technology used for keyboards, headsets, mice, and quick peripheral connections. Operates in the 2.4 GHz ISM band.
  • RFID (Radio Frequency Identification): uses tags and readers to identify and track items — inventory, access badges, asset tracking — at short to moderate ranges. Tags can be passive (no battery) or active (battery-powered, longer range).
  • NFC (Near Field Communication): extremely short-range (a few centimeters — tap to interact) used for tap-to-pay, quick Bluetooth pairing, and access control. Devices can act as both tag and reader.
  • These technologies solve different problems than Wi-Fi and are often used alongside Wi-Fi rather than replacing it.
Summary

Bluetooth, RFID, and NFC are common wireless technologies that serve very different purposes. Bluetooth connects personal devices and peripherals at short range. RFID focuses on identification and tracking using tags, while NFC supports near-contact interactions like payments and quick pairing. Knowing their range and purpose helps you choose the right technology and troubleshoot the correct layer.

A+ Exam Tip

Know these by range and use case: NFC = tap (centimeters), Bluetooth = room (meters), RFID = varies by type (passive tags = short range, active tags = longer range). Tap-to-pay = NFC. Wireless headset = Bluetooth. Inventory tracking tag = RFID.
